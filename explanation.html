What is Conway's Game of Life?

Conway's Game of Life is a cellular automaton devised by British mathematician John Horton Conway in 1970. Despite its name, it's not actually a game in the traditional sense—there are no players, and no winning or losing. Instead, it's a zero-player game, meaning its evolution is determined entirely by its initial state, requiring no further input from humans.

The Game of Life is a prime example of how simple rules can lead to incredibly complex and emergent behavior. It demonstrates how patterns can evolve, reproduce, move, and interact in ways that seem almost lifelike, despite being purely mathematical.

The Universe

The Game of Life takes place on an infinite two-dimensional grid of square cells. In our implementation, we use a finite grid for practical purposes, but the concept remains the same. Each cell can be in one of two states:

  • Alive (populated) - represented by a filled square
  • Dead (unpopulated) - represented by an empty square

Every cell interacts with its eight neighbors: the cells that are horizontally, vertically, or diagonally adjacent.

The Four Rules

At each step in time (called a generation), the following transitions occur simultaneously to all cells based on the current state:

Rule 1: Underpopulation

Any live cell with fewer than two live neighbors dies, as if by underpopulation.

Rule 2: Survival

Any live cell with two or three live neighbors lives on to the next generation.

Rule 3: Overpopulation

Any live cell with more than three live neighbors dies, as if by overpopulation.

Rule 4: Reproduction

Any dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes a live cell, as if by reproduction.

Important: All births and deaths occur simultaneously. The rules are applied to the entire grid at once, based on the current generation, to create the next generation.

🧬 Custom Rules & Cellular Automata Variations

While Conway's Game of Life is the most famous cellular automaton, it's just one of countless possible rule sets that can govern how cells live, die, and reproduce. Our Game of Life Studio lets you explore this vast universe of possibilities through custom rules.

Understanding the B/S Notation

Cellular automaton rules are typically written in B/S notation (Born/Survives):

  • B (Birth) - The neighbor counts that cause a dead cell to become alive
  • S (Survival) - The neighbor counts that allow a living cell to survive

For example, Conway's Game of Life is B3/S23:

  • B3 - Dead cells become alive with exactly 3 neighbors
  • S23 - Live cells survive with 2 or 3 neighbors

How Parameters Affect Behavior

Birth Rules (B)

Lower numbers (B0, B1, B2) create explosive growth - patterns spread rapidly and chaotically.

Higher numbers (B5, B6, B7, B8) create sparse patterns - only dense clusters can reproduce.

Survival Rules (S)

Few survival numbers (S1, S2) create unstable patterns - most structures quickly die out.

Many survival numbers (S12345678) create static patterns - once alive, cells rarely die.

Popular Rule Variations

Each rule creates a completely different "universe" with unique behaviors:

HighLife (B36/S23)

Like Conway's Life but with an extra birth condition. Creates replicating gliders and more complex growth patterns. Famous for its self-reproducing structures.

Seeds (B2/S)

Pure explosive chaos! Every pattern spreads rapidly because cells are born easily but never survive. Creates beautiful, organic-looking expanding patterns.

Mazectric (B3/S238)

Creates maze-like structures with long corridors and complex pathways. Patterns tend to form intricate, interconnected networks.

Move (B368/S245)

Patterns exhibit flowing, liquid-like behavior. Structures tend to move and reshape themselves constantly, rarely settling into stable forms.

Gnarl (B1/S1)

Creates fractal, organic patterns that grow slowly but persistently. Known for producing beautiful, tree-like or coral-like structures.

Replicator (B1357/S1357)

Patterns copy themselves in all four directions simultaneously, creating symmetric, expanding copies. Demonstrates perfect self-replication.

Mathematical Significance

Conway's Game of Life is mathematically significant because it is:

  • Turing Complete - It can simulate any computer algorithm
  • Undecidable - There's no algorithm that can predict whether a pattern will eventually die out
  • Chaotic - Small changes in initial conditions can lead to vastly different outcomes
  • Universal - Complex computers and logic gates can be built within the Game of Life

In fact, people have constructed entire computers, calculators, and even implementations of the Game of Life itself within the Game of Life!
